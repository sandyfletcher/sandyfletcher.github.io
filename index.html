HTML

In the 1980s, researchers at CERN were working with thousands of complex documents that all referenced each other. Employee and computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee proposed a system reliant on hypertext - links that allowed you to move through the network of academic papers with ease. The next iteration of his idea was Hypertext Markup Language (HTML), which would go on to become the structural backbone of the World Wide Web and modern internet.

HTML was designed to be simplistic, as Berners-Lee hoped the formatting would be adopted by non-technical users. The layout is almost essay-like, with header introducing basic information about the site, body containing the bulk of the page content, and footer leaving off with closing information and signatures.
